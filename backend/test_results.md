# Can authorities compel a person to produce documents that may incriminate them in financial or customs investigations? Discuss with reference to ECHR case law such as Funke v France.

In **Funke v France**, the applicant was convicted after refusing to disclose documents requested by customs authorities. The Strasbourg Court treated the compelled disclosure as engaging the right not to incriminate oneself, a fundamental aspect of the broader fair trial guarantee under Article 6(1), and linked to the privilege against self-incrimination recognised in domestic legal systems and international instruments.

Compelling document production in a way that forces a person to assist in their own incrimination in financial or customs matters can therefore violate Article 6.

The case also addressed arguments that objections to disclosure were premature because no formal charges had yet been brought. The ECHR rejected the idea that compelled production is harmless simply because incriminating facts have not yet been formally charged, showing that Article 6 protections apply even at investigative stages.

Overall, the coercive nature of disclosure duties is key: where authorities rely on compelling the individual to produce incriminating material rather than obtaining it independently, this risks breaching the privilege against self-incrimination.


# Can authorities compel a person to produce documents that may incriminate them in financial or customs investigations? Discuss with reference to ECHR case law such as Funke v France.

Whether authorities can compel a person to produce documents that may incriminate them in financial or customs investigations must be assessed in light of the ECHR right against self-incrimination under Article 6.

In Funke v France, the applicant was convicted for refusing to disclose documents demanded by customs authorities. The Court examined whether compulsory document production was compatible with fair trial guarantees.

The case establishes that the right not to give evidence against oneself is a general principle underlying Article 6(1). Compulsory disclosure duties engage this right where they effectively require the individual to provide material capable of leading to their own incrimination.

The ECHR approach indicates that authorities cannot bypass self-incrimination protections by compelling disclosure as a means of building their case. The legality of such measures depends on whether they respect the privilege against self-incrimination, particularly where the person is pressured to produce documents not already in the authorities’ possession.

The context of the investigation does not automatically justify compulsion; even at early stages, requiring disclosure that contributes to incrimination may violate Article 6.


# What is the legal significance of a case being struck out following a friendly settlement under Article 39 ECHR, and how was this applied in Michael Matthews v the United Kingdom?

Under Article 39 ECHR, when parties reach a friendly settlement and the Court is satisfied that it is based on respect for human rights, the case can be struck out of the list. This means the Court terminates the proceedings without delivering a judgment on the merits.

The legal significance is that there is no substantive determination of whether a Convention violation occurred. Instead, the Court’s role is limited to taking note of the agreement and ensuring it meets Convention standards. The settlement effectively resolves the dispute and typically includes undertakings such as compensation and waivers of further claims.

In Michael Matthews v the United Kingdom, the parties reached a friendly settlement under which the UK agreed to pay compensation and legal costs. The applicant waived further claims in respect of the matter. The Court took note of the agreement and struck the case out, meaning the application was disposed of without a merits ruling, consistent with the Article 39 mechanism.
